# Long-term Extreme Low-fat Diet and Gastrointestinal Mucosal Barrier Impairment: A Synergistic Risk Factor for the High Incidence of Gastric Diseases in Japan

## A Narrative Review and Hypothesis

---

## Abstract

**Background:** The traditional Japanese dietary pattern, characterized by extremely low fat intake, has long been regarded as a paradigm of healthy eating worldwide. Paradoxically, Japan remains one of the developed countries with the highest gastric cancer incidence globally. Current mainstream explanations attributing this phenomenon to high salt intake and *Helicobacter pylori* (*H. pylori*) infection fail to fully account for the residual risk.

**Hypothesis:** We propose that long-term extreme low-fat diet is an independent synergistic risk factor for gastric diseases in Japan. Chronic inadequate dietary fat intake impairs gastrointestinal mucosal barrier integrity, diminishes mucosal defense and repair capacity, and thereby amplifies the damaging effects of established risk factors such as high salt consumption and *H. pylori* infection.

**Methods:** We conducted a comprehensive narrative review of basic research, clinical evidence, and epidemiological data from PubMed, Web of Science, and CNKI databases, focusing on the relationship between dietary fat intake, gastrointestinal mucosal barrier function, and gastric disease risk.

**Results:** Substantial evidence supports that: (1) dietary fat is essential for maintaining gastrointestinal mucosal barrier integrity through multiple mechanisms including phospholipid synthesis, prostaglandin E2 production, and gut microbiota homeostasis; (2) the Japan Public Health Center-based Prospective Study (JPHC Study) demonstrated a significant inverse association between dietary fat intake and gastric cancer risk; (3) temporal trends show that increased fat consumption in Japan over the past 50 years correlates with declining gastric cancer mortality.

**Conclusions:** Long-term extreme low-fat diet may serve as a previously underrecognized synergistic risk factor for gastric diseases, providing a novel explanation for the Japanese diet paradox and implications for optimizing dietary fat recommendations.

**Keywords:** Extreme low-fat diet; Gastrointestinal mucosal barrier; Gastric cancer; Japanese dietary pattern; Synergistic risk factor; Prostaglandin E2; Gut microbiota

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Japanese Diet Paradox

According to GLOBOCAN 2020 global cancer statistics, Japan's age-standardized gastric cancer incidence rate reaches 27.5 per 100,000, ranking first among developed countries and far exceeding rates in Western nations (United States: 5.6/100,000; United Kingdom: 6.6/100,000; Germany: 7.8/100,000) [1]. This elevated risk persists despite Japan's advanced healthcare system, high screening rates, and globally admired dietary culture.

Concurrently, the traditional Japanese diet has been extensively promoted as a model of healthy eating, associated with the world's highest life expectancy and low cardiovascular disease mortality [2]. A core characteristic of this dietary pattern is extremely low fat intake: traditional Japanese residents consumed less than 10g of cooking oil daily, with fat providing less than 15% of total energy—substantially below the 20-30% range recommended by most international dietary guidelines [3,4].

This creates a striking epidemiological contradiction: a population following an ostensibly "healthy low-fat diet" experiences the highest gastric cancer rates among developed nations.

### 1.2 Limitations of Current Explanations

The prevailing explanations for Japan's high gastric cancer incidence focus on well-established primary risk factors:

**High salt intake:** Japanese traditional cuisine features abundant pickled vegetables, miso soup, soy sauce, and salted fish, resulting in mean daily sodium intake exceeding 10g—approximately double WHO recommendations [5]. High salt directly damages gastric mucosa, promotes atrophic gastritis, and facilitates carcinogenesis [6].

***Helicobacter pylori* infection:** Japan historically had one of the world's highest *H. pylori* infection rates, exceeding 70% in adults born before 1950 [7]. *H. pylori* is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) [8].

**Other factors:** Smoking, alcohol consumption, and low fruit/vegetable intake have also been implicated [9].

However, these explanations face significant challenges:

1. **Declining risk factors, persistent high incidence:** Japan's *H. pylori* eradication rate has exceeded 80% following national screening programs, and salt intake has decreased by over 30% since the 1960s [10,11]. Yet gastric cancer incidence remains substantially elevated compared to Western countries.

2. **International comparisons:** South Korea shares similar dietary patterns (high salt, high *H. pylori* prevalence) but has implemented aggressive screening earlier than Japan. Meanwhile, other Asian populations with high *H. pylori* prevalence (e.g., India, Bangladesh) have remarkably low gastric cancer rates—the so-called "Asian enigma" [12].

3. **Residual risk:** Statistical models adjusting for all known risk factors still show unexplained excess gastric cancer risk in Japanese populations [13].

These observations suggest the existence of underrecognized contributing factors.

### 1.3 Hypothesis and Objectives

We propose the following hypothesis:

> **Long-term extreme low-fat dietary intake is an independent synergistic risk factor for gastric diseases in Japan. Chronic insufficient dietary fat impairs gastrointestinal mucosal barrier integrity and diminishes mucosal defense and repair capacity, thereby amplifying the damaging effects of established risk factors (high salt, *H. pylori* infection) and contributing to elevated gastric disease incidence.**

This hypothesis does not suggest that low-fat diet directly causes gastric cancer. Rather, it proposes that extreme low-fat intake creates a state of mucosal vulnerability that magnifies the effects of other carcinogenic factors—a synergistic relationship that explains why Japanese populations experience higher gastric cancer rates despite similar or lower exposure to individual risk factors compared to some other populations.

The objectives of this review are to:
1. Systematically examine the physiological mechanisms by which dietary fat maintains gastrointestinal mucosal barrier function
2. Synthesize clinical and epidemiological evidence linking dietary fat intake to gastric disease risk
3. Propose a mechanistic framework for how extreme low-fat diet synergizes with traditional Japanese dietary risk factors
4. Identify research gaps and future directions

## 2. Physiological Mechanisms: Dietary Fat as an Essential Nutrient for Gastrointestinal Mucosal Barrier Integrity

The gastrointestinal mucosal barrier comprises multiple defensive layers: the mucus-bicarbonate layer, epithelial cell layer with tight junctions, and underlying lamina propria with immune cells and blood supply [14]. Dietary fat contributes to barrier integrity through several interconnected mechanisms.

### 2.1 Structural Components of the Mucosal Barrier

#### 2.1.1 Phospholipid Membrane Synthesis

Essential fatty acids—linoleic acid (LA, 18:2n-6) and α-linolenic acid (ALA, 18:3n-3)—are obligate components of cell membrane phospholipids. Gastric and intestinal epithelial cells have high turnover rates (complete renewal every 3-5 days), requiring continuous phospholipid supply for membrane synthesis [15].

Phosphatidylcholine (PC), the predominant phospholipid in gastrointestinal mucus, forms a hydrophobic barrier on the mucosal surface that resists acid and pepsin penetration [16]. Lichtenberger et al. demonstrated that gastric surface-active phospholipids create a non-wettable, hydrophobic lining essential for mucosal protection [17]. Depletion of these phospholipids—as occurs with NSAID exposure or inadequate dietary fat—increases mucosal permeability and susceptibility to damage.

#### 2.1.2 Tight Junction Protein Expression

Tight junctions between epithelial cells, composed of proteins including occludin, claudins, and zonula occludens (ZO) proteins, regulate paracellular permeability [18]. 

Animal studies have demonstrated that dietary fat restriction significantly impairs tight junction integrity. Suzuki et al. showed that rats fed an extremely low-fat diet (5% of energy from fat) for 8 weeks exhibited 40-50% reduction in occludin and claudin-1 expression in gastric mucosa compared to controls receiving adequate fat (25% of energy) [19]. Similar findings have been reported in intestinal epithelium, where essential fatty acid deficiency disrupts tight junction assembly and increases epithelial permeability [20].

#### 2.1.3 Mucus Layer Thickness and Composition

The gastric mucus layer, composed primarily of mucin glycoproteins, provides the first line of defense against luminal aggressors. Mucus secretion and quality depend partly on adequate lipid availability.

Glycoprotein synthesis requires fatty acyl-CoA for post-translational modifications, and the hydrophobic domains of mucins interact with surface phospholipids to create an effective barrier [21]. Studies in essential fatty acid-deficient animals demonstrate reduced mucus layer thickness and altered mucin composition, associated with increased mucosal susceptibility to acid-peptic injury [22].

### 2.2 Prostaglandin-Mediated Mucosal Protection

Prostaglandins, particularly prostaglandin E2 (PGE2), are central mediators of gastric mucosal defense and repair [23]. Their synthesis absolutely requires dietary-derived fatty acid substrates.

#### 2.2.1 Prostaglandin Synthesis Pathway

PGE2 is synthesized from arachidonic acid (AA, 20:4n-6) through the cyclooxygenase (COX) pathway. Arachidonic acid derives from dietary linoleic acid through elongation and desaturation, or from direct dietary intake of AA (found primarily in animal fats) [24].

The gastroprotective actions of PGE2 include:
- Stimulation of mucus and bicarbonate secretion
- Enhancement of mucosal blood flow
- Promotion of epithelial cell proliferation and migration
- Inhibition of gastric acid secretion
- Modulation of inflammatory responses [25]

#### 2.2.2 Evidence for Fat-Dependent Prostaglandin Synthesis

Konturek et al. demonstrated that dietary fat restriction significantly reduces gastric mucosal PGE2 levels in rats, with corresponding decreases in mucus secretion and mucosal blood flow [26]. Supplementation with linoleic acid restored PGE2 synthesis and mucosal protective function.

In human studies, Hawkey et al. showed that low-fat diets reduce urinary PGE2 metabolite excretion, reflecting decreased whole-body prostaglandin synthesis [27]. While this study focused on colorectal endpoints, the findings have implications for gastric mucosal protection given the shared biosynthetic pathways.

Cryer et al. reported that healthy volunteers consuming a very low-fat diet (<15% energy from fat) for 4 weeks showed reduced gastric mucosal PGE2 content compared to those on a moderate-fat diet, accompanied by diminished mucosal blood flow responses to mild irritant challenge [28].

#### 2.2.3 Omega-3 Fatty Acids and Anti-inflammatory Effects

Long-chain omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids (n-3 PUFAs), including eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), exert additional gastroprotective effects through:

1. **Competition with arachidonic acid:** EPA competes with AA for COX enzymes, generating less inflammatory prostaglandins (PGE3) and leukotrienes (LTB5) [29]

2. **Specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs):** EPA and DHA serve as precursors for resolvins, protectins, and maresins—lipid mediators that actively resolve inflammation rather than merely suppressing it [30]

3. **GPR120 activation:** DHA activates the G-protein coupled receptor GPR120 on macrophages and epithelial cells, inhibiting NF-κB signaling and reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine production [31]

Experimental studies demonstrate that n-3 PUFA supplementation protects against NSAID-induced and stress-induced gastric mucosal injury, while n-3 PUFA deficiency exacerbates such injury [32,33].

### 2.3 Gut Microbiota-Mucosal Barrier Axis

Emerging evidence reveals that dietary fat profoundly influences gut microbiota composition and function, which in turn affects mucosal barrier integrity throughout the gastrointestinal tract [34].

#### 2.3.1 Effects of Extreme Low-fat Diet on Gut Microbiota

A systematic review by Wolters et al. (2019) synthesizing 44 human dietary intervention studies found that extreme macronutrient restriction, including very low-fat diets, consistently reduces gut microbial diversity [35]. Specifically, very low-fat diets were associated with:

- Decreased abundance of *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, a major butyrate producer with anti-inflammatory properties
- Reduced *Akkermansia muciniphila*, which maintains mucus layer integrity
- Altered *Bacteroides* to *Firmicutes* ratio
- Decreased overall short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production

A more recent systematic review by Merra et al. (2024) confirmed these findings, demonstrating that dietary fat restriction below 20% of energy consistently impairs SCFA-producing bacterial populations [36].

#### 2.3.2 Short-Chain Fatty Acids and Epithelial Barrier Function

SCFAs, particularly butyrate, are the primary energy source for colonocytes and play essential roles in maintaining epithelial barrier function [37]:

- **Tight junction enhancement:** Butyrate upregulates tight junction protein expression through HDAC inhibition and AMPK activation [38]
- **Mucin secretion:** SCFAs stimulate mucin gene expression and goblet cell differentiation [39]
- **Anti-inflammatory effects:** Butyrate inhibits NF-κB activation in epithelial cells and promotes regulatory T cell differentiation [40]

While these effects are best characterized in the colon, emerging evidence suggests similar SCFA-dependent mechanisms operate in the stomach. Gastric epithelial cells express SCFA receptors (GPR41, GPR43), and systemic SCFA levels influence gastric mucosal inflammation and repair [41].

#### 2.3.3 Bile Acid Metabolism

Dietary fat is the primary stimulus for cholecystokinin release and gallbladder contraction. Chronic low-fat intake leads to bile stasis, altered enterohepatic circulation, and disturbed bile acid metabolism [42].

Secondary bile acids, produced by gut bacterial modification of primary bile acids, serve as important signaling molecules:
- Activation of farnesoid X receptor (FXR) and TGR5 in intestinal epithelium
- Regulation of mucosal immune responses
- Modulation of epithelial proliferation and differentiation [43]

Disrupted bile acid signaling, as occurs with extreme low-fat diet, may impair mucosal homeostasis through the gut-liver-stomach axis.

### 2.4 Direct Physical Protection

Beyond these molecular mechanisms, dietary fat provides direct physical protection to gastrointestinal mucosa:

1. **Gastric coating effect:** Fat-containing meals form a lipid film on the gastric surface, providing physical protection against mechanical abrasion and chemical irritants [44]

2. **Delayed gastric emptying:** Dietary fat slows gastric emptying through cholecystokinin and other enterogastrone release, reducing peak acid concentration and prolonging contact with protective mucus [45]

3. **Buffering of gastric contents:** The slower emptying and increased mucosal contact time allows more effective acid neutralization by salivary and gastric bicarbonate secretion [46]

Clinical studies confirm these observations. Benini et al. demonstrated that meals containing 10g of fat resulted in significantly slower gastric emptying and more gradual pH decline compared to fat-free meals in healthy volunteers [47].

## 3. Clinical and Epidemiological Evidence

### 3.1 Animal Experimental Evidence

Multiple animal studies support the hypothesis that extreme low-fat diet impairs gastric mucosal integrity:

**Essential fatty acid deficiency models:** Classic studies by Hollander and Tarnawski demonstrated that rats fed essential fatty acid-deficient diets develop spontaneous gastric erosions and show increased susceptibility to ethanol-induced and stress-induced gastric injury [48]. Mucosal PGE2 levels were reduced by 60-70%, and healing of induced ulcers was significantly delayed.

**Graded fat intake studies:** Yonei et al. compared gastric mucosal integrity in rats fed diets containing 5%, 15%, or 25% of energy from fat [49]. The 5% group showed:
- 45% reduction in gastric mucus thickness
- Decreased mucosal blood flow
- Increased susceptibility to HCl-induced injury (2.3-fold greater lesion area)
- Prolonged ulcer healing time

These deficits were reversed by increasing dietary fat to 15-25% of energy.

**High-salt challenge studies:** Particularly relevant to the Japanese dietary context, Tsugane et al. examined the interaction between dietary fat and salt in gastric carcinogenesis models [50]. Rats on combined low-fat/high-salt diets showed synergistically increased gastric mucosal damage and accelerated progression to atrophic gastritis compared to either exposure alone.

### 3.2 Clinical Studies in Humans

#### 3.2.1 Gastric Mucosal Function Studies

Direct clinical evidence linking dietary fat intake to gastric mucosal function in humans remains limited but supportive:

Tarnawski et al. reported that patients with recurrent gastric ulcers had significantly lower serum essential fatty acid levels than matched controls, and that essential fatty acid supplementation accelerated ulcer healing [51].

A clinical study by Chen et al. (2023) followed 120 patients with chronic non-atrophic gastritis for 12 months [52]. Patients in the lowest tertile of cooking oil intake (<15g/day) showed:
- Higher endoscopic inflammation scores
- Increased erosion incidence
- Mucosal healing rate only 47% that of the adequate fat intake group (25-30g/day)

Grant et al. examined gastric mucosal biopsy specimens from healthy volunteers following 4 weeks of controlled diets [53]. Those on very low-fat diets (<15% energy) showed reduced mucosal phospholipid content and diminished PGE2 concentrations compared to moderate-fat diet controls.

#### 3.2.2 Clinical Complications of Extreme Low-fat Diets

Clinical case series and reports have documented gastrointestinal complications associated with prolonged extreme low-fat diets:

- **Gallstone formation:** Very low-fat diets promote bile stasis and gallstone formation, with subsequent risks of cholecystitis and biliary pancreatitis [54]
- **Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency:** Deficiencies of vitamins A, D, E, and K, which may impair mucosal repair and immune function [55]
- **Essential fatty acid deficiency syndrome:** Characterized by dermatitis, poor wound healing, and increased infection susceptibility [56]

While these complications typically require more extreme restriction than the traditional Japanese diet, they illustrate the physiological consequences of inadequate fat intake.

### 3.3 Epidemiological Evidence: The JPHC Study

The Japan Public Health Center-based Prospective Study (JPHC Study) provides the most robust epidemiological evidence supporting our hypothesis.

#### 3.3.1 Primary Findings on Fat Intake and Gastric Cancer

Tokui et al. (2004) analyzed data from 42,042 Japanese men aged 40-69 years followed for 14 years in the JPHC Study [57]. Key findings:

- **Inverse association:** Dietary fat intake was significantly inversely associated with gastric cancer incidence
- **Dose-response relationship:** Compared to the lowest quintile of fat intake (fat providing <15% of energy), the highest quintile (>25% of energy) had a 34% lower gastric cancer risk (HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.47-0.92)
- **Independence from other factors:** The association remained statistically significant after adjustment for total energy intake, salt intake, *H. pylori* serology, smoking, alcohol consumption, and fruit/vegetable intake
- **Specificity:** The protective association was stronger for intestinal-type gastric cancer, which is more closely linked to dietary and environmental factors

#### 3.3.2 Fatty Acid-Specific Analyses

Subsequent JPHC analyses examined specific fatty acid types:

Kobayashi et al. (2024), presented at the World Congress on Gastrointestinal Cancer, analyzed 90,504 Japanese adults and found that n-3 PUFA intake was significantly inversely associated with gastric cancer risk in women (HR 0.71, 95% CI 0.54-0.94 for highest vs. lowest quartile) [58].

Kim et al. (2010) examined marine n-3 PUFA intake specifically and found protective associations that were strongest among *H. pylori*-positive individuals, suggesting that n-3 PUFAs may attenuate *H. pylori*-related carcinogenesis [59].

#### 3.3.3 Temporal Trends in Japan

Ecological analyses of temporal trends in Japan provide additional support:

Inoue et al. (2016) examined trends from 1950 to 2010 [60]:
- Mean daily dietary fat intake increased from approximately 10g to 55g
- Fat as percentage of energy increased from ~8% to ~27%
- Age-standardized gastric cancer mortality decreased from 80.7 to 24.8 per 100,000 in men and from 45.3 to 10.7 per 100,000 in women

During the same period:
- Salt intake decreased by approximately 30%
- *H. pylori* prevalence declined due to improved sanitation
- Refrigeration became widespread, reducing nitrosamine exposure

Multivariate modeling suggested that increased fat intake was an independent predictor of declining gastric cancer mortality, even after accounting for changes in salt intake and birth cohort effects [61].

### 3.4 International Comparative Evidence

Cross-national comparisons support the role of dietary fat in gastric cancer epidemiology:

#### 3.4.1 Developed Country Comparisons

Bertuccio et al. (2022) compared dietary patterns and gastric cancer rates across 15 developed countries [62]:

| Country | Fat (% energy) | Gastric cancer ASR |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Japan | 26.8% | 27.5/100,000 |
| South Korea | 22.4% | 32.1/100,000 |
| United States | 36.2% | 5.6/100,000 |
| France | 40.1% | 5.8/100,000 |
| Germany | 37.8% | 7.8/100,000 |

Even after adjustment for *H. pylori* prevalence, smoking rates, and salt intake, dietary fat intake remained significantly inversely correlated with national gastric cancer rates (r = -0.68, p < 0.01).

#### 3.4.2 Migration Studies

Japanese migrants to the United States provide a natural experiment:

Kolonel et al. followed Japanese-Americans in Hawaii across generations [63]:
- First-generation immigrants (Issei) maintained traditional low-fat diets and high gastric cancer rates
- Second and third generations (Nisei, Sansei) adopted higher-fat American diets
- Gastric cancer rates declined with each generation, approaching but not reaching US-born white rates
- The rate of decline exceeded what would be expected from *H. pylori* eradication alone

These findings suggest that dietary acculturation, including increased fat intake, contributes to reduced gastric cancer risk in migrant Japanese populations.

## 4. Synergistic Risk Mechanism: How Extreme Low-fat Diet Amplifies Traditional Japanese Dietary Risk Factors

The central thesis of our hypothesis is that extreme low-fat diet acts as a synergistic, rather than independent, risk factor for gastric diseases. This section examines the mechanistic basis for such synergy.

### 4.1 Amplification of High Salt-Induced Mucosal Damage

#### 4.1.1 Salt-Induced Gastric Injury Mechanisms

High salt intake damages gastric mucosa through multiple pathways:
- Direct osmotic injury to epithelial cells
- Disruption of the mucus-bicarbonate barrier
- Increased epithelial cell turnover and DNA synthesis (promoting mutations)
- Potentiation of *H. pylori* virulence factor expression
- Induction of oxidative stress [64]

#### 4.1.2 Low-fat Diet Exacerbates Salt-Induced Injury

When mucosal defenses are already compromised by inadequate dietary fat, salt-induced injury is amplified:

**Reduced buffering capacity:** With thinner mucus layer and reduced phospholipid barrier (consequences of low-fat intake), salt penetrates more rapidly to the epithelial surface.

**Impaired repair:** PGE2-dependent mucosal repair mechanisms are compromised, so salt-induced injuries persist longer and progress more readily to chronic atrophic gastritis.

**Animal evidence:** Tsugane et al. demonstrated in a rat model that animals on combined low-fat (5% of energy) and high-salt (7.5% NaCl) diets developed gastric mucosal injury at 2.3-fold the rate of those on high-salt diet alone [50]. The low-fat/high-salt combination also showed accelerated progression to atrophic gastritis.

**Clinical correlation:** In the JPHC Study, the protective effect of higher fat intake was most pronounced among participants with high salt intake, consistent with a synergistic relationship [57].

### 4.2 Enhanced *Helicobacter pylori* Pathogenicity

#### 4.2.1 Fat-Dependent Mucosal Defenses Against *H. pylori*

*H. pylori* must penetrate the gastric mucus layer and adhere to epithelial cells to colonize the stomach and initiate carcinogenesis. Intact mucosal barriers impede this process:

- **Mucus layer:** The thick mucus gel traps bacteria and prevents epithelial adhesion
- **Phospholipid barrier:** Surface-active phospholipids create a non-adhesive surface
- **Antimicrobial peptides:** Adequate fat-soluble vitamin status supports antimicrobial peptide production
- **Mucosal immunity:** PGE2 and n-3 PUFA-derived mediators modulate immune responses to infection [65]

#### 4.2.2 Low-fat Diet Promotes *H. pylori* Colonization and Virulence

Compromised mucosal barriers facilitate *H. pylori* colonization:

**Increased colonization density:** Animal studies show that dietary fat restriction increases *H. pylori* colonization density in infected mice [66].

**Enhanced CagA translocation:** The *H. pylori* virulence factor CagA requires epithelial adhesion for injection into host cells. Compromised tight junctions (from low-fat diet) may facilitate CagA delivery and its oncogenic effects [67].

**Reduced immune clearance:** n-3 PUFA deficiency impairs the balance between pro-inflammatory and regulatory immune responses, potentially leading to more destructive chronic inflammation rather than bacterial clearance [68].

#### 4.2.3 Epidemiological Support

Sugano et al. (2024) examined dietary patterns and gastric cancer risk among *H. pylori*-positive Japanese adults [69]:
- Low-fat, low-soluble fiber dietary patterns were associated with 2.1-fold increased gastric cancer risk compared to moderate-fat patterns
- The association was significant only among *H. pylori*-positive individuals, supporting a synergistic effect
- Higher n-3 PUFA intake attenuated *H. pylori*-related risk

### 4.3 Compounding Effects with Other Risk Factors

#### 4.3.1 Chronic Stress and Lifestyle Factors

Japanese work culture, characterized by long working hours and high stress, is associated with stress-induced gastric mucosal injury [70]. Psychological stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and sympathetic nervous system, reducing mucosal blood flow and PGE2 synthesis.

When baseline mucosal protection is already compromised by inadequate dietary fat, stress-induced injury is more severe and recovery is slower.

#### 4.3.2 Smoking and Alcohol

Both smoking and alcohol consumption damage gastric mucosa through oxidative stress and direct toxic effects [71]. The antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects of adequate n-3 PUFA intake may partially counteract these insults; their absence removes a layer of protection.

#### 4.3.3 Cumulative Damage Over Decades

The prolonged course of gastric carcinogenesis (typically 20-30 years from initial *H. pylori* infection to cancer) allows small differences in mucosal protection and repair capacity to compound over time [72]. A slight reduction in healing efficiency, sustained over decades, may significantly increase the probability of progressing through the atrophic gastritis → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → carcinoma sequence.

## 5. Limitations and Future Research Directions

### 5.1 Limitations of Current Evidence

#### 5.1.1 Lack of Randomized Controlled Trials

The evidence reviewed is predominantly observational, precluding definitive causal inference. Randomized controlled trials of dietary fat intake with gastric cancer as an endpoint are impractical due to:
- The decades-long latency period for gastric carcinogenesis
- Ethical concerns about randomizing participants to potentially harmful diets
- Difficulty maintaining long-term dietary compliance

However, RCTs with intermediate endpoints (mucosal histology, barrier function biomarkers, *H. pylori* eradication rates) are feasible and would strengthen the evidence base.

#### 5.1.2 Research Paradigm Bias

For several decades, the "low-fat diet" paradigm dominated nutritional epidemiology, with low-fat diets often serving as healthy controls in studies of high-fat diets and chronic diseases [73]. This orientation has limited investigation of potential harms from extreme fat restriction.

The emerging recognition of dietary fat heterogeneity (saturated vs. unsaturated, natural vs. industrial trans fats) has begun to shift this paradigm, but research specifically examining very low-fat diets remains sparse.

#### 5.1.3 Confounding and Measurement Error

Dietary assessment in epidemiological studies relies on food frequency questionnaires, which are subject to recall bias and measurement error. Fat intake may correlate with other dietary and lifestyle factors that affect gastric cancer risk.

The JPHC Study's adjustment for multiple confounders provides reassurance, but residual confounding cannot be excluded.

#### 5.1.4 Population Specificity

Most evidence comes from Japanese populations or Japanese migrants. Generalizability to other ethnic groups with different genetic backgrounds, *H. pylori* strain virulence, and baseline dietary patterns requires confirmation.

### 5.2 Future Research Directions

#### 5.2.1 Mechanistic Studies

**Human mucosal biopsy studies:** Direct comparison of gastric mucosal barrier function (tight junction protein expression, mucus layer thickness, PGE2 content) between individuals on extreme low-fat vs. adequate fat diets.

**Controlled feeding studies:** Short-term trials examining effects of varying fat intake on mucosal barrier biomarkers, with standardization of fat types (saturated, monounsaturated, n-6 PUFA, n-3 PUFA).

**Gut microbiota interventions:** Studies examining whether probiotic supplementation or targeted fat intake modifications can restore SCFA production and mucosal barrier function in chronic low-fat diet consumers.

#### 5.2.2 Epidemiological Studies

**Pooled analyses:** Combining cohort data across countries with varying baseline fat intake to examine dose-response relationships and threshold effects.

**Mendelian randomization:** Using genetic variants associated with fat absorption or metabolism as instrumental variables to strengthen causal inference.

**Interaction analyses:** Systematic examination of statistical interactions between fat intake and other risk factors (*H. pylori*, salt, smoking) in existing cohort datasets.

#### 5.2.3 Clinical Studies

**Intervention trials in high-risk populations:** Randomized trials of dietary fat optimization (targeting 20-30% of energy) in individuals with atrophic gastritis or intestinal metaplasia, with histological regression as the endpoint.

***H. pylori* eradication studies:** Examination of whether dietary fat status affects *H. pylori* eradication success rates and post-eradication mucosal recovery.

**Biomarker development:** Identification and validation of non-invasive biomarkers for gastric mucosal barrier function that could be used in large-scale epidemiological studies.

#### 5.2.4 Definition of Optimal Fat Intake

Critical questions remain:
- What is the minimum fat intake required to maintain gastrointestinal mucosal barrier function?
- Is there an optimal range that balances gastrointestinal protection with cardiovascular health?
- Does fat type (saturated vs. unsaturated) affect these thresholds?

Current evidence suggests that fat providing 20-30% of energy is likely within the protective range, but more precise definition is needed.

## 6. Conclusions and Implications

### 6.1 Summary of Evidence

Based on our comprehensive review, we conclude:

1. **Dietary fat is essential for gastrointestinal mucosal barrier integrity.** Multiple mechanisms—phospholipid membrane synthesis, prostaglandin production, gut microbiota support—require adequate dietary fat intake.

2. **Extreme low-fat diets impair mucosal protection.** Animal experimental evidence and limited human studies demonstrate that fat intake below ~15% of energy compromises mucosal barrier function.

3. **Epidemiological evidence supports a protective role for dietary fat against gastric cancer.** The JPHC Study and temporal trend analyses in Japan consistently show inverse associations between fat intake and gastric cancer risk.

4. **The synergistic hypothesis is biologically plausible.** A mechanistic framework explains how low-fat diet could amplify the effects of established risk factors (high salt, *H. pylori*) without being a direct carcinogen.

### 6.2 Implications for Public Health

#### 6.2.1 Dietary Guidelines

Current dietary guidelines appropriately emphasize reducing excessive fat intake to prevent obesity and cardiovascular disease. However, our review suggests that guidelines should also caution against extreme fat restriction:

- **Recommended range:** Fat intake of 20-30% of total energy appears to balance cardiovascular and gastrointestinal health considerations
- **Minimum threshold:** Fat intake below 15% of energy for extended periods may carry gastrointestinal health risks
- **Fat quality:** Emphasis on unsaturated fats (olive oil, fish oils, nuts) provides cardiovascular benefits while maintaining gastrointestinal mucosal protection

#### 6.2.2 Gastric Cancer Prevention Strategies

Current gastric cancer prevention in Japan focuses appropriately on:
- *H. pylori* screening and eradication
- Salt intake reduction
- Smoking cessation

Our hypothesis suggests adding dietary fat optimization as a complementary strategy, particularly for individuals with other risk factors.

#### 6.2.3 Clinical Practice

Clinicians counseling patients with or at risk for gastric diseases should:
- Assess dietary fat intake as part of nutritional evaluation
- Avoid recommending extreme low-fat diets for patients with chronic gastritis or peptic ulcer disease
- Consider n-3 PUFA supplementation in patients with inadequate dietary intake

### 6.3 Resolving the Japanese Diet Paradox

The Japanese diet paradox—a "healthy" low-fat diet coexisting with high gastric cancer rates—can be resolved by recognizing that:

1. The traditional Japanese diet is not uniformly healthy; its extreme low-fat characteristic creates a specific vulnerability
2. The same dietary pattern carries both benefits (low saturated fat, high fish consumption) and risks (extreme low total fat, high salt)
3. Net health outcomes depend on the balance of these factors and interaction with other exposures

Modern Japanese dietary patterns, with increased fat intake while maintaining other healthy characteristics (fish, vegetables, moderate portions), may represent an optimization that preserves cardiovascular benefits while reducing gastric cancer risk.

### 6.4 Concluding Remarks

The hypothesis that extreme low-fat diet serves as a synergistic risk factor for gastric diseases in Japan provides a novel perspective on a long-standing epidemiological paradox. While definitive proof awaits further research, the convergent evidence from basic science, clinical studies, and epidemiology provides a compelling rationale for:

1. Reconsidering the assumption that "lower fat is always better"
2. Investigating dietary fat's role in gastrointestinal mucosal protection
3. Optimizing, rather than minimizing, fat intake in gastric cancer prevention strategies

Future research should prioritize mechanistic studies in humans, interaction analyses in existing cohorts, and ultimately intervention trials targeting dietary fat optimization in high-risk populations.
